Dense retrieval encodes queries and documents as continuous vectors in a shared
embedding space. Semantic similarity is measured by cosine distance: documents
that are conceptually related to the query score highly even if they share no
exact terms. This handles synonyms, paraphrasing, and language variation well.

Sparse retrieval, typically BM25 over an inverted index, excels at exact keyword
matching. A query containing a specific product code, acronym, or technical term
reliably surfaces documents containing that exact string, independently of
semantic similarity. Sparse methods degrade on paraphrase but dominate on
vocabulary mismatch in the query's favour.

Hybrid retrieval combines both signals. Reciprocal Rank Fusion assigns each
document a score of 1 / (k + rank) for every ranked list it appears in, then
sums those scores. Documents that rank well in dense search and in sparse search
accumulate higher combined scores than documents strong in only one list.

The practical gain is coverage: dense retrieval catches semantically similar
documents that sparse misses; sparse retrieval catches exact-match documents that
dense misses. RRF merges both without requiring score normalisation, because it
operates on ranks rather than raw similarity scores.
