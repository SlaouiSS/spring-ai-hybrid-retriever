Sentence-BERT (SBERT) adapts BERT for semantic sentence similarity by fine-tuning
on sentence pairs with a siamese network structure. Each sentence is encoded
independently by a shared BERT encoder, and the resulting representations are
compared by cosine similarity.

The bi-encoder architecture is what makes dense retrieval scalable: document
embeddings are computed once at index time and stored. At query time only the
query is encoded, and similarity is computed against all cached document vectors.
This contrasts with cross-encoders, which process query and document jointly
and cannot precompute representations.

Mean pooling over the final layer's token embeddings typically outperforms using
only the [CLS] token for sentence similarity tasks. The all-MiniLM-L6-v2 model
uses mean pooling and produces 384-dimensional vectors trained specifically for
semantic textual similarity.

SBERT models are trained on large corpora of natural language inference and
semantic textual similarity datasets. The training objective pushes semantically
similar sentences close together in the embedding space and dissimilar ones apart,
measured by cosine distance.
