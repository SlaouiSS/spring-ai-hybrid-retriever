Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF) was introduced by Cormack, Clarke, and Buettcher
in 2009 as a simple, parameter-light method for combining ranked lists from
heterogeneous retrieval systems. The score for a document d is:

  RRF(d) = Σ  1 / (k + rank(d, list))
          lists

where rank is 1-based and k is a smoothing constant, defaulting to 60. Documents
not appearing in a given list contribute nothing to the sum for that list.

RRF operates on ranks, not on raw scores. This makes it robust to the fact that
cosine similarity scores and BM25 scores live on completely different scales. No
normalisation, calibration, or tuning is required before fusion.

A document that appears at rank 1 in both the dense and sparse lists scores
2 / (k + 1) ≈ 0.0328 for k = 60 — exactly double the score of a document that
appears at rank 1 in only one list. This doubling effect is the formal expression
of the fusion value: overlap between retrieval signals is rewarded.

Disjoint lists — where no document appears in both — produce a simple interleaving
of results with no true fusion. The RRF score degrades gracefully to a weighted
interleave in this case, still better than discarding one list entirely.
