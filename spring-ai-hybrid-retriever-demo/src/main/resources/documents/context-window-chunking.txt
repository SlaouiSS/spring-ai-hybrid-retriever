Large language models process input within a finite context window measured in
tokens. Inserting too many retrieved documents exhausts the token budget and can
degrade generation quality as the model struggles to attend to all the content
uniformly.

Document chunking splits long texts into smaller segments before indexing. The
optimal chunk size depends on the embedding model's maximum sequence length and
the granularity of information the retrieval step must resolve. Chunks that are
too small lose surrounding context; chunks that are too large dilute the relevant
signal and increase the chance of a low-relevance passage dominating the chunk's
embedding.

Overlap between consecutive chunks — repeating the last N tokens of a chunk at
the start of the next — reduces the risk of splitting a sentence or concept that
spans a chunk boundary.

Cross-encoder reranking can further improve precision after the initial retrieval
step. A reranker reads the query and each candidate passage jointly and produces
a relevance score, unlike bi-encoder embedding models that encode query and
passage independently. Reranking is computationally heavier but more accurate;
applying it to the top-20 candidates before feeding the top-5 to the context
window is a common production pattern.
